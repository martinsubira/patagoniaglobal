Why Travel Patagonia Argentina and Chile Together?

Patagonia is not two destinations. It is one vast, untamed region shared by Argentina and Chile, where geography ignores political borders and nature unfolds as a continuous, dramatic landscape.

For international travelers, this means one thing: the opportunity to experience two distinct cultures and ecosystems within a single journey. Instead of choosing between countries, the smartest way to explore Patagonia is to combine them.

Those who plan a cross-border itinerary unlock a richer, more complete version of the region. On the Argentine side, the landscape stretches wide and open, shaped by endless steppe, glacial lakes, and legendary peaks like Mount Fitz Roy. Distances feel vast, the horizon uninterrupted, and the experience deeply connected to remoteness and scale.

Cross into Chile, and Patagonia transforms. The terrain becomes more vertical, carved by water and time into fjords, dense forests, and some of the most iconic protected areas in South America, including Torres del Paine National Park. Here, the landscapes feel compressed, intense, and cinematic.

This contrast is what defines Patagonia. It is not about choosing one side over the other, but understanding how both complement each other.

Combining Argentina and Chile in one itinerary allows travelers to experience Patagonia as it truly exists: diverse, interconnected, and far more compelling than any single-country trip. It also maximizes long-haul travel investment, turning a once-in-a-lifetime journey into a deeper, more efficient exploration.

In a region where distances are vast and logistics can be complex, thinking beyond borders is not just inspiring. It is the key to unlocking the ultimate Patagonia experience.

Best Patagonia Argentina Chile Itinerary Routes

Lake District Route

Easy + Scenic

⏱ 7–10 days · Best for: First-time visitors

  1. San Carlos de Bariloche Argentina
    Commonly known as Bariloche, it is a major tourist destination in Argentina's Río Negro Province, located in the Andes foothills beside Lake Nahuel Huapi. It serves as the gateway to Patagonia's lake district.
  2. Cross into Chile via Cardenal Samoré Pass
  3. Puerto Varas Chile
    A scenic city on the shores of Lake Llanquihue, known for its German-influenced architecture and spectacular views of the Osorno and Calbuco volcanoes. A major hub for Chilean Patagonia tourism.

Glacier & Trekking Route

Most Popular

⏱ 10–14 days · Best for: Hikers and photographers

  1. El Chaltén Argentina
    A small mountain village beneath Monte Fitz Roy and Cerro Torre — internationally known as Argentina's trekking capital. Nestled at the confluence of the Río de las Vueltas and Río Fitz Roy, it sits within Los Glaciares National Park surrounded by glacial lakes, lenga forests, and rugged granite spires.
  2. El Calafate Argentina
    A Patagonian city on the southern shore of Lago Argentino — Argentina's largest freshwater lake — and the main gateway to Los Glaciares National Park (UNESCO). Its turquoise glacial waters, Andean backdrop, and world-class glacier access make it one of Patagonia's most visited destinations.
  3. Cross border → Torres del Paine National Park Chile
    One of South America's most iconic wilderness areas — granite towers, massive glaciers, and turquoise lakes shaped by the Southern Patagonian Ice Field. The W Trek and O Circuit are among the world's great long-distance hikes. Gateway: Puerto Natales (112 km south).

Southern Patagonia & Carretera Austral

For Advanced Travelers

⏱ 2–3 weeks · Best for: Advanced travelers

  1. Ushuaia Argentina
    A port city on the southern coast of Argentina's Tierra del Fuego archipelago. Known as the world's southernmost city, it serves as the gateway to Antarctic expeditions and Patagonia's subpolar wilderness.
  2. Explore Tierra del Fuego National Park
  3. Carretera Austral Chile
    A 1,240-km scenic highway traversing fjords, forests, rivers, and glaciers — the only continuous land route through northern Patagonia. Renowned worldwide for its pristine landscapes and outdoor adventure.

How to Get to Patagonia

Patagonia has no single gateway — it depends on your route. Most international travelers enter through Buenos Aires (Argentina) or Santiago (Chile), then connect to regional airports.

International gateways

  • Buenos Aires — Ezeiza (EZE) — Main international hub for Argentina. Connections to all Patagonian destinations.
  • Santiago — Arturo Merino Benítez (SCL) — Main international hub for Chile. Direct connections to Punta Arenas and Puerto Montt.

Domestic airlines — Argentina

  • Aerolíneas Argentinas — Widest network. Flies to Bariloche (BRC), El Calafate (FTE), Ushuaia (USH), Puerto Madryn (PMY), Neuquén (NQN) and more.
  • LATAM Argentina — Flies Buenos Aires to Bariloche, El Calafate, and Ushuaia.
  • Flybondi — Low-cost. Buenos Aires to Bariloche, Neuquén, and other destinations.
  • JetSmart Argentina — Low-cost. Growing Patagonian network from Buenos Aires and Córdoba.

Domestic airlines — Chile

  • LATAM Chile — Main carrier. Santiago to Punta Arenas (PUQ) and Puerto Montt (PMC) daily.
  • Sky Airline — Santiago to Puerto Montt and Punta Arenas. Good fares with advance booking.
  • JetSmart Chile — Low-cost. Santiago to Punta Arenas and Puerto Montt.

Key Patagonian airports

  • BRC — Bariloche — Gateway to the Lake District. 15 km from city center.
  • FTE — El Calafate — Gateway to Los Glaciares and Torres del Paine. 23 km from El Calafate.
  • USH — Ushuaia — Southernmost commercial airport. 4 km from city center.
  • PMC — Puerto Montt — Gateway to Carretera Austral and Lake District Chile. Connects to Puerto Varas (60 km).
  • PUQ — Punta Arenas — Gateway to Torres del Paine and Magallanes. 20 km from city center.

National Parks of Patagonia

Patagonia holds some of the most protected and biodiverse wilderness on Earth. Both Argentina and Chile maintain extensive national park networks — most require entry fees and some demand advance reservations.

Argentina

  • Parque Nacional Los Glaciares — UNESCO World Heritage. Home to Perito Moreno Glacier, El Chaltén, and El Calafate. Entry fee applies. Reservation required for Laguna de los Tres in high season.
  • Parque Nacional Nahuel Huapi — Argentina's oldest national park (1934). Based in Bariloche. Free entry. Lakes, forests, and glaciers in the northern Lake District.
  • Parque Nacional Tierra del Fuego — Argentina's only national park with Atlantic coast access. 12 km from Ushuaia. End of the world trails and Lapataia Bay.
  • Parque Nacional Los Alerces — UNESCO World Heritage (2017). Ancient Alerce trees up to 2,600 years old. Near Esquel, Chubut.

Chile

  • Parque Nacional Torres del Paine — Chile's most visited park. Biosphere Reserve. Entry fee: ~USD 35 (high season). W Trek and O Circuit require advance booking months ahead.
  • Parque Nacional Pumalín Douglas Tompkins — Created from private conservation land donated by the Tompkins family. Carretera Austral, Aysén. Ancient Alerce forests and fjords.
  • Parque Nacional Queulat — Home to the spectacular Ventisquero Colgante (Hanging Glacier). Carretera Austral, Aysén. One of the most visually dramatic parks in Chile.
  • Parque Nacional Bernardo O'Higgins — Chile's largest national park. Accessible only by boat from Puerto Natales. Remote glaciers and fjords south of Torres del Paine.

Getting Around: Car Rentals & Transport

Patagonia rewards independent travelers. A rental car gives access to remote valleys, viewpoints, and stops that buses miss entirely. Plan ahead — vehicles are limited and roads vary widely.

Key tips for renting in Patagonia

  • Cross-border permission — Most rental companies in Argentina allow crossing into Chile, but you must request authorization in advance and pay a fee (~USD 50–100). Confirm before signing — not all companies permit it.
